Overview

Evolution is the story of life changing through time: populations gradually shift in their traits because of variation, heredity, and differential success. Think of populations as decks of cards (the gene pool) — shuffling, drawing, and swapping cards over generations produces new combinations of traits. The main processes that shuffle and sort those cards are mutation (new cards), natural selection (which cards win the game), genetic drift (random shuffling), migration/gene flow (cards moving between decks), and recombination (mixing cards during reproduction).

Big idea: evolution does not have a direction or final goal — it is a continual response to environments, constraints, and chance. The modern synthesis connects Darwin's natural selection to Mendel's genetics so we can predict how allele frequencies change over time.

Mendelian Genetics

Gregor Mendel discovered that traits are passed as discrete units (genes) and that each individual carries two versions (alleles) of a gene. Simple Mendelian inheritance makes predictions about offspring when alleles interact in predictable ways. Dominant alleles mask recessive ones in a heterozygote; codominance and incomplete dominance are variants where both alleles influence the phenotype.

  • Alleles and genotype: two copies per individual, inherited one from each parent.
  • Dominance patterns: complete dominance, incomplete dominance, codominance — each produces distinct phenotypic outcomes.

Punnett squares are a quick way to visualize how parental alleles combine. For example, Aa × Aa produces AA, Aa, Aa, aa genotypes — a predictable 1:2:1 genotypic ratio even as phenotypes depend on dominance relationships.

Extensions

When multiple genes influence traits (polygenic inheritance) or genes are linked on the same chromosome, inheritance patterns become richer. Linkage reduces independent assortment and generates non-Mendelian ratios unless crossing-over recombines alleles.

Hardy–Weinberg Equilibrium & Allele Frequencies

Hardy–Weinberg is a null model: if nothing interesting is happening (no selection, mutation, migration, drift, and mating is random), allele and genotype frequencies stay constant. For a simple two-allele system, p and q describe allele frequencies and p², 2pq, q² predict genotype frequencies. Deviations from the prediction indicate that evolutionary forces are acting.

p + q = 1
p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1

Why it matters: use Hardy–Weinberg to estimate carrier frequencies from phenotypes (e.g., if q² is known), and as a statistical baseline — significant departure suggests evolution at that locus. Remember assumptions — reality often violates one or more, which is why the model is practically useful.

Worked example

If 9% of a population shows the recessive phenotype (aa), q² = 0.09 so q = 0.3 and p = 0.7. Expected heterozygotes = 2pq = 0.42 (42%).

Natural Selection & Adaptation

Natural selection is the non-random increase of beneficial traits. Individuals do not evolve — populations do, as heritable trait variants that increase survival or reproduction rise in frequency. Fitness (w) quantifies reproductive success; the selection coefficient (s) describes how much less fit a genotype is relative to a reference (w = 1 − s).

Selection comes in flavors: directional (favors one extreme), stabilizing (favors intermediates), and disruptive (favors extremes). Real-world adaptation is a balance between selection, mutation creating raw material, gene flow mixing variation, and drift introducing chance.

Phylogenetics & Tree Building

Phylogenetics reconstructs evolutionary relationships — who is more closely related to whom — using shared traits (morphological or molecular). A phylogenetic tree is a hypothesis about ancestry and branching order. Homologous traits reflect shared ancestry; analogous traits arise by convergence and can mislead naive analyses.

Methods range from quick distance-based approaches to rigorous model-based inference (maximum likelihood, Bayesian) that explicitly model how sequences change. Parsimony seeks the tree with the fewest changes; model-based methods weigh different evolutionary scenarios and often give better results for molecular data.

Important: topology (branching order) is the main message of a tree; branch lengths may encode genetic change or time depending on the analysis.

Speciation & Macroevolution

Speciation is the splitting of one lineage into two reproductively isolated lineages. Allopatric speciation occurs when geographic barriers separate populations. Sympatric speciation can occur without physical separation (e.g., polyploidy in plants), and parapatric speciation happens along environmental gradients. Over long timescales these splitting events accumulate into macroevolutionary patterns visible in the fossil record and tree of life.

Isolating mechanisms can act before fertilization (prezygotic: behavioral, temporal, mechanical, gametic) or after (postzygotic: reduced hybrid viability or fertility). Speciation often involves an interplay of selection, drift, and changes in mating systems or chromosome number.

Evidence for Evolution

Multiple independent lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion: life has evolved. The fossil record shows transitional forms and changing assemblages through time. Comparative anatomy reveals homologous structures and vestigial traits. Biogeography explains distributions through history and plate tectonics. Molecular biology provides direct measures of relatedness via DNA and protein similarities. Finally, contemporary examples — antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, and lab evolution experiments — show evolution happening now.

  • Fossils: snapshots of past forms, including transitional morphologies.
  • Comparative anatomy: homologous structures and vestiges tell a story of descent.
  • Molecular data: sequences let us quantify relatedness and timing.
  • Direct observation: rapid evolution in response to strong selective pressures.

Further Reading & Resources

If you want to dig deeper, classic textbooks like Futuyma's Evolution and Hartl & Clark's Principles of Population Genetics are excellent. Online resources — NCBI, Tree of Life — and primary literature will expand your view. Practice is key: work Punnett squares, solve Hardy–Weinberg problems, and try building small phylogenetic trees from mock sequence data to see the concepts in action.

  • Textbooks: 'Evolution' by Futuyma; 'Principles of Population Genetics' by Hartl & Clark.
  • Web: NCBI, Tree of Life, educational tutorials on phylogenetics and population genetics.
  • Activities: hands-on Punnett squares, Hardy–Weinberg calculations, simple phylogenetic reconstructions in MEGA or online tools.
